Introduction 1.1. Background and motivation In recent years, there has been a growing concern about the extent to which human biases can be incorporated into artificial intelligence (AI) systems, leading to harmful con- sequences. As the amount of big data increases, optimizing AI systems solely for performance will not only fail to im- prove real-world applications but also increase the risk of bias in ways that may not be immediately apparent. For example, recent studies have revealed similar issues with fairness in machine learning systems [4]. Related work Several methods have been proposed to help AI models understand data on a level that is more similar to real-world scenarios, thereby allowing the data to remain unprocessed and reducing the risk of bias ( [1] and [2]). These methods require protected attributes, which are attributes that should not be used in decision-making to avoid bias and discrimina- tion. However, in real-world applications, it is often unclear which attributes should be protected, and there are very few benchmarks for this kind of problem. A recent study introduced controlled bias using Meta Al- bum, which used different backgrounds as a form of bias (Meta-Album, 2022). The authors created a biased dataset with 90% of the images in a dominant group with biased backgrounds and the remaining 10% in a minimal group. They found that when presented with an unbiased test set, the worst group accuracy dropped dramatically, indicating that the model was biased by backgrounds. However, the process of fusing different backgrounds required manual work and could not be scaled up to create larger datasets for a reliable benchmark.
