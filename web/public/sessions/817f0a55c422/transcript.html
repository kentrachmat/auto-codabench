Introduction 1.1. Background and motivation In recent years, there has been a growing concern about the extent to which human biases can be incorporated into artificial intelligence (AI) systems, leading to harmful con- sequences. As the amount of big data increases, optimizing AI systems solely for performance will not only fail to im- prove real-world applications but also increase the risk of bias in ways that may not be immediately apparent. For example, recent studies have revealed similar issues with fairness in machine learning systems [4]. Therefore, it is our responsibility to design AI systems that are bias-invariant and prioritize fairness. Research on the fairness of AI has been growing, and various methods for reducing bias are being developed. As a result, there is a strong need for more benchmarks to incentives researchers to make significant contributions in this field. 1.2. Related work Several methods have been proposed to help AI models understand data on a level that is more similar to real-world scenarios, thereby allowing the data to remain unprocessed and reducing the risk of bias ( [1] and [2]). These methods require protected attributes, which are attributes that should not be used in decision-making to avoid bias and discrimina- tion. However, in real-world applications, it is often unclear which attributes should be protected, and there are very few benchmarks for this kind of problem. A recent study introduced controlled bias using Meta Al- bum, which used different backgrounds as a form of bias (Meta-Album, 2022). The authors created a biased dataset with 90% of the images in a dominant group with biased backgrounds and the remaining 10% in a minimal group. They found that when presented with an unbiased test set, the worst group accuracy dropped dramatically, indicating that the model was biased by backgrounds. However, the process of fusing different backgrounds required manual work and could not be scaled up to create larger datasets for a reliable benchmark. Additionally, this method only works for animals or natural objects with bias in the background because of their environment and cannot be generalized for other domains such as text, microscopic images, textures, etc. To address these limitations, we have found a different way to add controlled bias to a dataset, called Neural Style Trans- fer. This method can be generalized to all kinds of domains and requires little to no manual work when creating syn- thetic images. 2. Problem Formalization and Overview 2.1. Neural Style Transfer Algorithm We use Neural Style Transfer to introduce a different pro- tected attribute to our dataset calledstyle. This style attribute can then be used to create groups with imbalanced protected attributes, thus producing bias for our data. Neural Style Transfer [3] uses VGG-19 to reconstruct the style and content of input images in the following steps: • First, it generates a white noise image as initialization called generated image. • Then, both the content image and the generated im- age are passed through the pre-trained VGG-19 model. The squared-error content loss between the two feature representations is then defined: Lcontent = LX l=0 wl  X ij (F l ij − P l ij)2   (1) where F l is the output of content image at layers l, P l is the output of generated image at layers l and wl is the weight for each layer indicating how much a layer contributes to the content loss • To reconstruct style, we also pass both the style image and generated image through VGG-19. The squared- error style loss between the two feature representations is then defined: Lstyle = LX l=0   wl 4N 2 l M 2 l X ij (Gl ij − Al ij)2   (2) where Al is the Gram matrix of the output ofgenerated image at layers l, Gl is the Gram matrix of the output of style image at layers l, wl is the weight indicating the degree to which that layer contributes to the style loss, Nl is the number of channels of that layer, and Ml is the total number of features - the product of the channel’s height and width - for each channel of that layer. • Finally, we minimize the total loss function: Ltotal = αLcontent + βLstyle (3) with β α = 100 and using L-BFGS as optimizer because we get the best results from these particular settings.
